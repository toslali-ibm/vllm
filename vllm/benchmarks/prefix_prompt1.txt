Let’s consider the Global Positioning System (GPS), a utility we often take for granted. It works through a process called trilateration. A constellation of at least 24 satellites orbits the Earth, each one constantly broadcasting a signal that contains its precise location and the exact time the signal was sent. 
Your phone's GPS receiver picks up signals from multiple satellites. By measuring the minuscule difference in time it takes for each signal to arrive, it can calculate its distance from each satellite. The Magnus effect is the observable phenomenon commonly associated with a spinning object moving through a fluid (like air). When a ball is spinning as it flies, one side of the ball is moving in the same direction as the air flowing past it, while the other side is moving in the opposite direction. This creates a pressure differential, with higher pressure on the side moving against the airflow and lower pressure on the side moving with it. This imbalance generates a net force that causes the ball to curve away from its initial trajectory. This is the secret behind a pitcher's curveball, a soccer player's bending free-kick, and a tennis player's topspin. It’s a beautiful example of fluid dynamics dictating the outcome of a game.
